The Apollo 11 mission was the spaceflight that first landed humans on the Moon. Commander Neil Armstrong and lunar module pilot Buzz Aldrin formed the American crew that landed the Apollo Lunar Module Eagle on July 20, 1969.

The Saturn V Rocket
The Saturn V was an American human-rated super heavy-lift launch vehicle used by NASA between 1967 and 1973. It was a three-stage liquid-fueled super heavy-lift launch vehicle developed to support the Apollo program. The Saturn V remains the tallest, heaviest, and most powerful rocket ever brought to operational status. It generated 7.5 million pounds of thrust at liftoff.

The Lunar Descent
The descent to the lunar surface was perilous. As Eagle descended, the guidance computer triggered several program alarms (1202 and 1201), which indicated that the computer was overloaded with radar data. However, mission control determined it was safe to continue. Armstrong had to take semi-manual control as the automated targeting system was guiding them into a boulder-filled crater, landing with only 25 seconds of fuel remaining.

The Moonwalk
After landing, Armstrong descended the ladder and proclaimed: "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind." Aldrin joined him shortly after, describing the view as "magnificent desolation." They collected 47.5 pounds (21.5 kg) of lunar surface material, deployed scientific instruments including a seismic experiment package, and planted the US flag. They spent about two and a quarter hours outside the spacecraft.

The Return Journey
After spending 21 hours and 36 minutes on the lunar surface, Eagle's ascent stage launched back into lunar orbit to rendezvous with the Command Module Columbia, piloted by Michael Collins. Collins had remained alone in orbit, orbiting the moon 30 times. The crew returned to Earth and splashed down safely in the Pacific Ocean on July 24, 1969, fulfilling President Kennedy's goal of reaching the moon before the end of the decade.
